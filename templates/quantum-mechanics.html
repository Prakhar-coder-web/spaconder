First - What is Quantum mechanics? Quantum mechanics is a fundamental theory in physics that provides a description of the physical properties of nature at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles. It explains the behavior of matter and its interactions with energy on the scale of atomic and subatomic particles. It deals with the behavior of matter and light on the atomic and subatomic scale. So basically it means study of the properties and behavior of smaller particles like atoms and its subatomic particles - Electrons, Protons and Neutrons.

Quantum entanglement: It basically states that when two waves interact with each other, they get connected such that even if you take them apart from each other, one will affect another. Like if two particles interact with each other and now I take them 1010 Km far apart and rotate one particle, the other will also rotate. Einstein was very uncomfortable about this property of Quantum Mechanics. Imagine two particles interacting with each other and I take them several light years apart. If I will move the first particle, the other particle will also move in the same way, which implies, that I can instantly know about the behaviour of the second particle which is many light years apart, and that means information is travelling faster than speed of light which doesnt agree with classical physics laws.

Quantum tunneling: Suppose you throw a ball and it hits a wall. Either the ball will come to a stop or the ball will bounce back. At least this is what happens in classical physics. In quantum mechanics these rules kind of go haywire. A particle or more specifically a wave can pass through a barrier. Now this thing might look simple but in reality it is not. There is a lot more complex theory and equations behind it, but we will cover just the fundamental part.

The quantum tunneling effect is a quantum phenomenon that occurs when particles move through a barrier that, according to the theories of classical physics, should be impossible to pass through. The barrier may be a physically impassable medium. Upon encountering a barrier, a quantum wave will not end abruptly; rather, its amplitude will decrease exponentially. This drop in amplitude corresponds to a drop in the probability of finding a particle further into the barrier.

If the barrier is thin enough, then the amplitude may be non-zero on the other side. This would imply that there is a finite probability that some of the particles will tunnel through the barrier. The tunneling current is defined as the ratio of the current density emerging from the barrier divided by the current density incident on the barrier.
