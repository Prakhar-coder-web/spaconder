Heisenberg Uncertainty principle

Heisenberg's uncertainty principle states that it is impossible to measure both the position and momentum of the particle with precision at the same time or simultaneously. If you measure the position of the particle or object accurately, then you won't be able to measure its momentum with precision at the same time. If you measure the momentum of the particle with precision then your knowledge of the position of the particle would be uncertain. It can be ignored in the macroscopic world. So if you are trying to apply this principle on a ball, then good luck because the uncertainty would be exiguous to be noticeable. It is significant at the sub-atomic level where the increased accuracy of the velocity of a particle would result in increased uncertainty about the position and Vice-Versa.

Why can't we measure the position and the momentum of the particle with absolute precision?

The principle is based on wave-particle duality. It is not that we don't have advanced technology to do. It's not related to the instruments used to measure. It's because such information does not exist in nature. The information of a particle's absolute position and momentum does not exist in nature simultaneously. To measure one property, the other has to become uncertain. Let's have a look at this graph.

This graph does have a defined wavelength but now since it's a wave, it is spread out so it doesn't have an absolute position. Hence, we know about the momentum of the particle but are uncertain about the position

Momentum is only defined when a wave has a wavelength, which means it doesn’t have a position (because a wavelength means the wave is “repeated” over space), and position is only defined when a wave doesn’t have a wavelength and thus doesn’t have momentum (because having a position means that it isn’t spread out over space).
